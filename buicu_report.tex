% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent I present BUICU, a fully Bayesian forecasting system for
intensive care unit crowding. Using conjugate Gamma--Poisson inference,
BUICU maintains a continuously updated belief distribution over the
patient arrival rate~$\lambda$ and propagates posterior uncertainty
through 2,000-trajectory Monte Carlo simulation to produce calibrated
occupancy forecasts. On 180 days of synthetic MIMIC-IV--calibrated data
featuring two surge windows, a windowed Bayesian model outscores the
stationary model by 39.4 log-predictive units, a gap entirely
attributable to regime changes. A Law-of-Total-Variance decomposition
reveals that after sufficient observation, 99.4\% of forecast variance
is irreducible Poisson noise, while sensitivity analysis demonstrates
that bed capacity (not arrival-rate estimation) is the dominant driver
of crowding risk. I deploy the model as an interactive Streamlit
application and systematically audit five failure modes, yielding a
combined $\times 2.27$ confidence-interval widening factor that
stretches the crowding probability range from a point estimate of
41.5\% to an honest interval of 18--94\%.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Intensive care units operate at the margin. A single surge (flu season,
a mass-casualty event, a cluster of post-surgical complications) can
push a 50-bed unit from comfortable to catastrophic overnight. The human
cost of being caught off-guard is measured in delayed surgeries, hallway
beds, exhausted staff, and preventable deaths. Despite this, most
hospital census projections remain deterministic: they predict
\emph{a number} rather than \emph{a distribution}, projecting false
confidence at precisely the moments when uncertainty matters most.

What is needed is a probabilistic forecast that is \emph{honest about
what it does not know}. BUICU (Belief Updating for ICU Crowding Under
Uncertainty) addresses three questions that any evidence-based capacity
planner must answer:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the current patient arrival rate $\lambda$, and how
          confident is that estimate?
    \item Given the current census, what is the probability of exceeding
          nominal capacity within the next 48~hours?
    \item Which modelling assumptions matter most, and where does the
          model break down?
\end{enumerate}

The answer to (1) is exact Bayesian conjugate inference; the answer to
(2) is Monte Carlo simulation that propagates every source of
uncertainty; and the answer to (3) is a systematic failure-mode audit
that compounds five identified limitations into a single, defensible
uncertainty-widening factor. I demonstrate all three via an interactive
web application that invites the reader to explore the model before
(or instead of) reading the mathematics.

% ── 2. Methods ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data Generation}

All experiments use a synthetic dataset generated by a configurable
MIMIC-IV--calibrated simulator. Arrivals follow a \emph{non-homogeneous
Poisson process} with diurnal and weekly modulation, produced via the
\textbf{Lewis--Shedler thinning algorithm}. Two surge windows
(days 30--50 and 110--130, rate $1.8\times$ baseline) introduce
non-stationarity. Length of stay is drawn from a two-component
log-normal mixture (70\% short acute, 30\% long chronic), calibrated to
published MIMIC-IV statistics (median $\approx 2$ days,
mean $\approx 4$ days). Approximately 2\% of discharge timestamps are
randomly censored to simulate realistic data-quality issues. The
generator is fully parameterized: arrival rate, surge windows, capacity,
LOS distribution, and random seed are all configurable, so every result
can be reproduced under alternative scenarios without modifying the
inference pipeline.\footnote{Synthetic data is used for ethical reasons:
real ICU records contain protected health information.}

\subsection{Probabilistic Model}

Let $k_t$ denote the number of admissions on day~$t$. I model each
count as conditionally Poisson:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
    N_t \mid \lambda \;\sim\; \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda),
\end{equation}
an assumption grounded in the independence and memorylessness of
individual patient deterioration events.\footnote{This independence
assumption is violated during infection waves and trauma surges;
Section~4.1 quantifies the impact.} I place a conjugate
\textbf{Gamma prior} on the unknown rate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prior}
    \lambda \;\sim\; \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_0,\, \beta_0),
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_0 = 2$, $\beta_0 = 0.2$, giving a prior mean of~10
admissions/day and a standard deviation of~$\approx 7.1$, deliberately
wide to let the data speak. The Gamma--Poisson conjugacy yields a
closed-form posterior after $T$ days of observation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:posterior}
    \lambda \mid k_{1:T} \;\sim\; \mathrm{Gamma}\!\left(
        \alpha_0 + \textstyle\sum_{t=1}^T k_t,\;\;
        \beta_0 + T
    \right).
\end{equation}
Each morning the update reduces to $\alpha \leftarrow \alpha + k_t$,
$\beta \leftarrow \beta + 1$. No Markov chain Monte Carlo, no
variational approximation is needed; the update is algebraically exact.
The posterior mean $\alpha/\beta$ drifts toward the empirical mean while
the variance $\alpha/\beta^2$ shrinks monotonically, providing a
built-in measure of estimation confidence.

\subsection{Posterior Predictive Distribution}

The natural next question, \emph{how many patients should one expect
tomorrow?}, has a Bayesian answer that integrates out residual
parameter uncertainty rather than ignoring it:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:predpred}
    P(N_{\text{fut}} = k) = \int_0^\infty \!\mathrm{Pois}(k;\lambda)
    \, \mathrm{Gamma}(\lambda;\alpha,\beta)\, d\lambda.
\end{equation}
This integral evaluates to the \textbf{Negative Binomial} distribution
with parameters $r = \alpha$ and $p = \beta/(\beta + \Delta t)$. The
NegBin is overdispersed relative to a Poisson at the posterior mean:
its variance reflects \emph{both} the irreducible Poisson randomness
\emph{and} the remaining uncertainty about $\lambda$. After 180~days,
the posterior predictive 95\% interval for tomorrow's count is
$[8,\, 16]$, wider than a fixed-$\lambda$ Poisson would suggest,
and appropriately so.

\subsection{Windowed Model for Non-Stationarity}

The stationary model treats all 180~days of history equally. This is
convenient but dangerous: a two-week surge that ended a month ago still
pulls the posterior toward the elevated rate. To let the model
\emph{forget}, I restrict the sufficient statistics to the most recent
$W = 14$ days:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:windowed}
    \alpha_t^{(W)} = \alpha_0 + \!\!\sum_{s=t-W+1}^{t} k_s, \quad
    \beta_t^{(W)} = \beta_0 + W.
\end{equation}
This remains exact conjugate inference over a truncated dataset. The
trade-off is explicit: smaller windows react faster but have higher
variance; larger windows are more stable but slower to detect regime
changes. I adjudicate with the \textbf{one-step-ahead log predictive
score}, a proper scoring rule that cannot be gamed by miscalibrated
predictions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:logscore}
    S_T = \sum_{t=2}^{T}
    \ln\,\mathrm{NegBin}(k_t;\, \alpha_{t-1},\, p_{t-1}).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Occupancy Forecasting}

The arrival rate determines how many patients enter the ICU; it says
nothing about how long they stay. Occupancy at time~$t$ is
$O_t = \sum_i \mathbf{1}[a_i \le t < a_i + L_i]$, where $a_i$ is
the admission time and $L_i$ is the length of stay (LOS). Because
$a_i$, $L_i$, and the total count are all random, $O_t$ is a random
variable whose distribution cannot be obtained analytically.

For each of $S = 2{,}000$ Monte Carlo trajectories I:
(i)~sample $\lambda^{(s)}$ from the Gamma posterior;
(ii)~draw future arrivals from $\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda^{(s)} \Delta t)$
    with uniform random arrival offsets;
(iii)~bootstrap $L_i^{(s)}$ from the empirical LOS distribution; and
(iv)~overlay the $n_0$ patients already in beds (deterministic, since
    they are currently observed) to compute $O_t^{(s)}$
    for $t \in [0,\, 48]$~hours.
The result is a full predictive distribution over occupancy at every
future time step, including quantile bands for operational
decision-making.

\subsection{Uncertainty Decomposition}

A critical question for resource planning: how much of the forecast
uncertainty could be eliminated by collecting more data? The
\textbf{Law of Total Variance} provides a precise decomposition:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:totvar}
    \mathrm{Var}[N] =
    \underbrace{E[\mathrm{Var}[N \mid \lambda]]}_{\text{aleatoric}}
    + \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}[E[N \mid \lambda]]}_{\text{epistemic}}.
\end{equation}
Both terms have closed forms: the aleatoric component is
$\Delta t \cdot \alpha/\beta$ (irreducible Poisson noise); the
epistemic component is $\Delta t^2 \cdot \alpha/\beta^2$ (uncertainty
about~$\lambda$ that shrinks with more data).

% ── 3. Results ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}

\subsection{Arrival-Rate Estimation and Prior Robustness}

The posterior converges to $\hat\lambda = 11.72$ admissions/day
(95\% credible interval $[11.23,\, 12.23]$). To test robustness, three
priors are run through the same data: \emph{uninformative}
$(\alpha_0, \beta_0) = (0.01, 0.001)$, \emph{weakly informative}
$(2, 0.2)$, and \emph{strong but wrong} $(50, 10)$ (prior mean~5).
After 180~days, pairwise KL divergences between all three posteriors
fall below $10^{-3}$~nats, confirming the Bernstein--von~Mises theorem
in action. The ``wrong'' prior required $\sim$15~days to be overwritten
by the data, a useful calibration of how quickly evidence overwhelms
belief.

\subsection{Model Comparison}

The windowed model outscores the stationary model by \textbf{39.4
log-score units}. The gap is entirely attributable to surge periods,
where the stationary model assigns systematically low probability
to elevated counts. Outside surges, the two models perform comparably.
I also compare MLE $\hat\lambda_\text{MLE} = \sum k / T$ against the
full Bayesian posterior. Early in the data (days 1--20), the Bayesian
credible interval is substantially wider than the MLE-based confidence
interval, correctly reflecting the scarcity of evidence; as data
accumulates, the two converge. Posterior predictive $p$-values flag
17 of 180~days as anomalous ($p < 0.025$ or $p > 0.975$); 94\% of
these fall within the surge windows, confirming the model's ability to
detect genuine distributional surprises.

\subsection{Uncertainty Decomposition}

After 180~days the posterior has $\alpha \approx 2100$,
$\beta \approx 180$. The epistemic fraction evaluates to:
\[
    \frac{\Delta t/\beta}{1 + \Delta t/\beta}
    = \frac{1/180}{1 + 1/180}
    \approx 0.6\%.
\]
\textbf{99.4\% of forecast uncertainty is irreducible Poisson noise.}
No amount of additional data can reduce it. The only way to narrow the
predictive interval is to change the underlying process, for example
through triage protocols, regional load-balancing, or elective-surgery
scheduling. This is arguably the most practically important finding:
the bottleneck is not statistical but operational.

\subsection{Crowding Forecast and Sensitivity Analysis}

Forecasting from day~36 (a near-capacity snapshot at the onset of the
first surge, with 38 of 50~beds occupied) yields a 48-hour crowding
probability of $\mathbf{41.5\%}$ (bootstrap 95\% CI: 40--43\%), with
peak mean occupancy reaching 44~patients.

administrators where to focus: \emph{protecting bed availability matters
more than refining the statistical model}.

% ── 4. Discussion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Failure-Mode Audit}

A model that does not know how it fails is dangerous. I
systematically audit five failure modes, each following the chain
\emph{assumption $\to$ violation $\to$ consequence $\to$ mitigation}:

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{FM1: Non-stationarity.} The Poisson model
assumes constant~$\lambda$; real surge/normal ratio is~1.76. The
windowed model mitigates this (CI~$\times 1.23$).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{FM2: Independence violations.} Lag-1
autocorrelation $r = 0.08$; LOS--occupancy coupling ignored. A Hawkes
process would address this (CI~$\times 1.02$).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{FM3: Informative censoring.} 2.1\% missing
discharge timestamps bias LOS downward. Kaplan--Meier correction
required (CI~$\times 1.04$).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{FM4: Heavy tails.} LOS excess kurtosis of
5.8 means rare 20$+$\,day stays are undersampled by bootstrap.
Generalized Pareto tail fit needed (CI~$\times 1.14$).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{FM5: Goodhart's Law.} If staff divert
patients based on forecasts, the model learns the \emph{managed}
rate and underpredicts risk when interventions are removed, creating a
dangerous oscillation. Cannot be empirically detected; flagged
unconditionally (CI~$\times 1.05$).

\smallskip\noindent The five penalties compound multiplicatively to a
\textbf{combined widening factor of $\times 2.27$}, stretching the
crowding probability interval to 18--94\%, a stark reminder that a
41\% point estimate carries substantial structural uncertainty beyond
sampling variability.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}

The discharge model is the weakest link: LOS is sampled independently
of occupancy, ignoring the well-documented \emph{boarding effect}.
Incorporating $L_i(\text{occupancy})$ would sharpen the crowding
forecast. The Poisson arrival model neglects temporal clustering that a
\emph{Hawkes process} would capture. The windowed model discards
historical context that a \emph{changepoint prior} or particle filter
could retain. Finally, validation on real de-identified MIMIC-IV data
is needed before any clinical deployment.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

Three safeguards are non-negotiable. First, only synthetic data is used.
Second, every forecast is a distribution: the website never says
``there will be 12 admissions''; it says ``expect 8--16 with 95\%
probability.'' Third, failure modes are front-page content on the
website, not a buried footnote. A model that informs staffing decisions
in critical care and cannot articulate how it fails should not be
trusted.

% ── 5. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

BUICU demonstrates that conjugate Bayesian inference, combined with
Monte Carlo simulation and a rigorous failure-mode audit, produces
probabilistic crowding forecasts that are both computationally trivial
and epistemically honest. The central practical finding, that
\emph{bed capacity, not model refinement, is the dominant lever for
crowding risk}, reframes the problem from a statistical question to
an operational one. The interactive deployment at
\url{https://buicu-cs109.streamlit.app} makes this argument tangible:
visitors can manipulate priors, watch beliefs update in real time,
stress-test capacity scenarios, and inspect every assumption. A
forecast that carries a credible interval, detects its own anomalies,
and discloses its failure modes is more useful than a deterministic
prediction that projects false confidence.

\section{Mathematical Derivations}\label{app:math}

\subsection{Conjugate Update}

The Gamma--Poisson conjugacy follows from combining the Poisson
likelihood $P(k \mid \lambda) = \lambda^k e^{-\lambda}/k!$ with the
Gamma prior $p(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{\alpha_0 - 1} e^{-\beta_0 \lambda}$.
The posterior is proportional to:
\[
    p(\lambda \mid k_{1:T}) \;\propto\;
    \lambda^{\alpha_0 + \sum k_t - 1}\,
    e^{-(\beta_0 + T)\lambda}
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    \mathrm{Gamma}\!\left(\alpha_0 + \textstyle\sum k_t,\; \beta_0 + T\right).
\]
The posterior mean $\alpha/\beta$ converges to the sample mean
$\bar{k}$ as $T \to \infty$; the posterior variance
$\alpha/\beta^2 = O(1/T)$ contracts at the parametric rate.

\subsection{KL Divergence Between Successive Posteriors}

For two Gamma distributions $P_{\text{new}} = \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_n, \beta_n)$
and $P_{\text{old}} = \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_o, \beta_o)$:
\begin{align}
    \mathrm{KL}(P_\text{new} \| P_\text{old})
    &= (\alpha_n - \alpha_o)\,\psi(\alpha_n)
    - \ln\Gamma(\alpha_n) + \ln\Gamma(\alpha_o) \nonumber\\
    &\quad + \alpha_o\bigl(\ln\beta_n - \ln\beta_o\bigr)
    + \alpha_n\,\frac{\beta_o - \beta_n}{\beta_n},
\end{align}
where $\psi$ is the digamma function. This closed-form expression
quantifies the information gained from a single day's observation.

\subsection{Full Variance Decomposition}

For $N \mid \lambda \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda \Delta t)$ with
$\lambda \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$:
\begin{align}
    E[\mathrm{Var}[N \mid \lambda]]
    &= \Delta t \cdot \alpha / \beta
    &&\text{(aleatoric)} \\
    \mathrm{Var}[E[N \mid \lambda]]
    &= \Delta t^2 \cdot \alpha / \beta^2
    &&\text{(epistemic)}
\end{align}
The epistemic fraction $(\Delta t / \beta)\big/(1 + \Delta t / \beta)$
vanishes as $\beta \to \infty$, i.e., with more data.

\section{Failure-Mode Details}\label{app:failure}

\noindent\textbf{FM1: Non-stationarity.}\quad
The Poisson model assumes a constant $\lambda$. The real ICU has surge
windows where the arrival rate is $1.8\times$ baseline. The stationary
posterior averages across regimes, underestimating crowding risk during
surges and overestimating it during lulls. \emph{Detection:} a surge/normal
ratio of 1.76 exceeds the 1.3 threshold. \emph{Mitigation:} the windowed
model. \emph{CI widening:} $\times 1.23$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{FM2: Independence violations.}\quad
The Poisson model treats each time window as independent. A lag-1
autocorrelation of $r = 0.08$ in daily counts is detected, indicating
mild clustering. More seriously, LOS tends to increase during crowding
(boarding effect in real hospitals). \emph{Mitigation:} Hawkes process
or autoregressive Poisson. \emph{CI widening:} $\times 1.02$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{FM3: Data quality.}\quad
2.1\% of discharge timestamps are missing, not at random. Long-stay
patients are more likely to have missing records (informative
right-censoring), biasing the empirical LOS distribution downward.
\emph{Mitigation:} Kaplan--Meier survival analysis. \emph{CI widening:}
$\times 1.04$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{FM4: Heavy tails.}\quad
The LOS distribution has excess kurtosis of 5.8, indicating that a few
patients stay 20+ days. These rare but long stays create sustained
periods of high occupancy that the empirical bootstrap may undersample.
\emph{Mitigation:} Generalized Pareto fit to the upper tail; importance
sampling. \emph{CI widening:} $\times 1.14$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{FM5: Goodhart's Law.}\quad
If ICU staff use crowding forecasts to divert patients or accelerate
discharges, the observed arrival rate reflects the \emph{managed} system,
not the counterfactual. The model learns the managed rate and will
underpredict crowding if interventions are removed, creating a dangerous
oscillation. This failure mode cannot be empirically detected; I flag it
unconditionally. \emph{CI widening:} $\times 1.05$.

\medskip\noindent The five penalties compound multiplicatively, yielding a
\textbf{combined CI widening factor of $\times 2.27$}. Applied to the
48-hour forecast, the model-limitation-adjusted interval for
P(overcrowded) is 18\%--94\%.

\section{CS\,109 Concepts Demonstrated}\label{app:concepts}

This project touches 16 concepts from the CS\,109 curriculum:

\medskip\noindent\textit{Probability Foundations:} Random variables
($N_t$, $L$, $O_t$, $\lambda$); probability distributions (Poisson,
Gamma, Negative Binomial, LogNormal); conditional probability
$P(N_t \mid \lambda)$; Bayes' theorem applied as a conjugate update rule.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Bayesian Inference:} Posterior predictive
distribution; conjugate priors with exact closed-form updates; Law of
Total Variance (epistemic/aleatoric decomposition); Monte Carlo
simulation (2,000+ trajectories).

\medskip\noindent\textit{Frequentist Methods:} Maximum likelihood
estimation $\hat{\lambda}_\text{MLE} = \Sigma k / T$; Central Limit
Theorem for frequentist confidence intervals; KL divergence for
information-gain quantification; hypothesis testing via posterior
predictive $p$-values.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Model Evaluation:} Proper scoring rules
(one-step-ahead log predictive score); calibration via coverage
probabilities and probability integral transform histograms; sensitivity
analysis mapping assumptions to conclusions; prior sensitivity
demonstrating posterior convergence.

\section{Interactive Website Architecture}\label{app:website}

The Streamlit application at \url{https://buicu-cs109.streamlit.app} is
structured as a narrative scroll:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Interactive hook:} A slider invites users to guess the
          arrival rate before any data is revealed. After locking in a
          guess, the posterior mean is exposed alongside a comparison
          verdict.
    \item \textbf{Prior explorer:} Visitors manipulate $\alpha_0$ and
          $\beta_0$ sliders and watch the prior--posterior comparison
          update in real time.
    \item \textbf{Sequential update trajectory:} A 180-day animation
          scrubber shows the posterior evolving day by day, with KL
          divergence and anomaly flags displayed at each step.
    \item \textbf{Crowding forecast:} Capacity and horizon sliders
          recompute the Monte Carlo estimate on demand (cached with
          \texttt{@st.cache\_data}). A sensitivity bar chart shows
          P(overcrowded) at 80\%, 100\%, and 120\% capacity.
    \item \textbf{Model evaluation tab:} Three-panel view comparing
          stationary vs.\ windowed trajectories, MLE vs.\ Bayesian
          intervals, and an expandable failure-mode accordion with
          detection evidence and severity ratings.
\end{enumerate}
